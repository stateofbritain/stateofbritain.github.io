# Policy Challenges — Editorial Spec

## Purpose

The Policy Challenges tab maps the legal and regulatory frameworks that constrain government action on persistent policy problems. It does not argue for or against any position. It shows what the rules are, how they connect, and what the trade-offs of changing them would be.

## Tone

Match the rest of the site exactly. The data pages work because they are **neutral, precise, and trust the reader**. The policy pages must do the same with legal and regulatory content.

### Do

- State what the law says, citing the specific article or section
- Show connections between instruments as factual dependencies ("Article 524 of the TCA references the ECHR")
- Present trade-offs symmetrically — every "if X" gets both the gain and the cost, without weighting either
- Let scale speak for itself (e.g. show Dublin returns data alongside crossing data — the reader sees the disproportion without being told it's a "myth")
- Use the same measured language you'd use in a briefing note for someone who hasn't made up their mind
- Attribute contested claims ("Critics argue...", "The government's position is...")

### Don't

- Editorialize ("failed", "myth", "red herring", "broken", "the real question")
- Frame questions to imply an answer ("Why can't the UK...?" implies it should be able to)
- Use rhetorical devices (dramatic reveals, "boom", building to a punchline)
- Take a position on whether a constraint is good or bad
- Use sarcasm or loaded juxtapositions ("£700m — zero flights")
- Second-guess political motivations ("political cover", "no government has been willing")

### Tone test

Before publishing, read each sentence and ask: "Would a senior civil servant presenting to a cross-party select committee say this?" If not, rewrite it.

## Page structure

Each policy page follows a consistent structure:

### 1. Title and summary (2-3 sentences)
What area this covers and what makes it structurally complex. No value judgement on whether the status quo is good or bad.

**Example**: "The UK's asylum and immigration framework involves international treaties, bilateral agreements, and domestic statutes with significant interdependencies. Changes to one instrument can have consequences for others."

### 2. Context metrics (MetricCard row)
3-5 key numbers that set the scale. Raw data, sourced, no editorial framing.

**Example**: "Channel crossings: 36,816 (2024)" — not "Channel crossings: 36,816 — and rising"

### 3. Context charts
Small multiples showing relevant trends. Let the reader compare scales visually rather than telling them what to notice.

### 4. The legal framework
Layered by type (international → bilateral → domestic). Each instrument shows:
- **Name, year, status** — factual
- **Summary** — what it does, in one paragraph
- **Key provisions** — specific articles/sections with quoted or paraphrased text
- **Effect on policy** — what this instrument means for government action in this area. Neutral description of the constraint, not a judgement on whether the constraint is desirable

Colour-coding by type (international treaty, bilateral agreement, domestic statute, no longer in force).

### 5. Dependencies
Specific connections between instruments, presented as factual relationships:
- "The TCA references the ECHR in Article 524"
- "The HRA incorporates ECHR rights into domestic law via Section 1"
- "The Illegal Migration Act's core removal duty (Section 2) has been repealed"

Each dependency chain shows the sequence of consequences if a change is made, with both sides of the trade-off.

### 6. Scenarios ("What changes if...")
Structured as: trigger → list of consequences by area, each tagged as:
- **Expanded scope** (government gains new options)
- **Reduced scope** (government loses existing capability)
- **Uncertain** (outcome depends on negotiation or implementation)

Not "positive/negative" — that implies a value judgement. What's positive depends on your priorities.

### 7. Timeline
Key dates in the evolution of the framework. Factual events only, no characterisations.

### 8. Sources
Links to primary legislation (legislation.gov.uk), treaty texts, and official statistics.

## Data standards

- All legal references cite specific articles, sections, or clauses
- Statistics are sourced to official publications (Home Office, ONS, UNHCR)
- Where data is contested or estimated, say so
- Use legislation.gov.uk links for all UK statutes
- Use official treaty texts for international instruments

## What this section is NOT

- A think-tank report arguing for reform
- A fact-check debunking political claims
- A manifesto comparison
- Legal advice

It is a reference map. The reader decides what to do with it.
