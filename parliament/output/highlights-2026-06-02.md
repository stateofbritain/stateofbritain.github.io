# Hansard Highlights — 2026-06-02

*Commons · 15 highlights · compiled 2026-06-03T14:47:09Z*

> The Commons sitting on 2 June 2026 was dominated by a statement on the murder of 18-year-old Henry Nowak, in which the Home Secretary committed to a fully resourced Independent Office for Police Conduct investigation, rejected calls to review the kirpan religious exemption, and refused to frame the case as one of preferential treatment of communities. An urgent question on the Milburn interim report on young people not in education, employment or training, departmental questions to the Energy Secretary set against the war in Iran and a 13 per cent price cap rise, the Committee stage of the Armed Forces Bill on housing, justice and reserves, and an adjournment debate on systemic Department for Work and Pensions errors affecting Scottish carers filled the rest of the day.

### 1. Crisis — Shabana Mahmood

**Debate:** Murder of Henry Nowak
**Time:** 2026-06-02T13:44:01

**Why it matters.** The Home Secretary, Shabana Mahmood, said the Independent Office for Police Conduct will be resourced to investigate the police response to Henry Nowak's death, ruled out collective blame on the Sikh community, and set the family's call against further division as the test for the wider response.

> “We do not believe in collective punishment in this country. Instead, we stand together against an act of pure evil.”

Mahmood spoke the day after Vickrum Digwa was sentenced to life with a minimum 21-year term, with further charges authorised against members of his family and Digwa's mother convicted of assisting an offender. She paired the IOPC commitment with the Government's halving knife crime plan, citing a 10 per cent fall in knife crime and a 27 per cent fall in knife homicides to a decade low, and named misinformation online and threats against police officers as a danger that the House should jointly condemn. She also flagged that the Offensive Weapons Act 2019 protections for the kirpan are not being reopened.

### 2. Effective scrutiny — Chris Philp (Con, Croydon South)

**Debate:** Murder of Henry Nowak
**Time:** 2026-06-02T13:51:53

**Why it matters.** The shadow Home Secretary, Chris Philp (CON), pressed the Home Secretary to withdraw the College of Policing's March 2025 'police anti-racism commitment', saying it directs forces to equalise arrest rates between ethnic groups despite differing offending rates.

> “The police anti-racism commitment, published in March 2025 by the National Police Chiefs’ Council and the College of Policing, urges police forces to reverse-engineer arrest rates for ethnic groups so that those rates are the same, even though the offending rates are different, by treating different ethnic groups differently.”

Philp linked the police response in the Nowak case to the College of Policing document, and to the Valdo Calocane case in Nottingham and the Axel Rudakubana case in Southport, where the Opposition argues that race-based assumptions affected risk assessments. The Home Secretary did not agree to withdraw the document, noting that the race action plan for the police began under the previous Conservative Government and that policing by consent requires confidence from all communities. Sir Julian Lewis (CON) put the same withdrawal request to her later in the statement.

### 4. Policy signal — Andrew Western

**Debate:** Milburn Review: Interim Report

**Why it matters.** The Parliamentary Under-Secretary, Andrew Western, told the Commons that £2.5 billion in youth-guarantee funding will deliver half a million opportunities for young people to work, train or take apprenticeships, in response to Alan Milburn's interim report on those not in education, employment or training.

> “During their last few years in power, the number of young people not in education, employment or training rose by a quarter of a million—a shameful legacy.”

Western said the report shows the problem is 'deep-seated and long-term' rather than a feature of the last year or two, and read paragraph 266 of the report into the record to rebut Conservative claims that recent national insurance changes had driven the rise. He set out the Government's response: a youth guarantee with referral to one of six options at three months, a £3,000 grant for small and medium-sized enterprises hiring apprentices, and a £2,000 grant for employers hiring a young person who has been on universal credit for six months or more. The full Milburn recommendations are expected later in the year.

### 6. Policy signal — Shabana Mahmood

**Debate:** Murder of Henry Nowak
**Time:** 2026-06-02T14:39:08

**Why it matters.** The Home Secretary, Shabana Mahmood, ruled out launching a formal review of the religious exemption that allows Sikhs to carry the kirpan, saying she would speak first to the Sikh community, knife crime campaigners and the police.

### 14. Casework — Josh Babarinde (LD, Eastbourne)

**Debate:** Armed Forces Bill
**Time:** 2026-06-02T16:09:34

**Why it matters.** Josh Babarinde (LD) told the House that his late constituent Pauline Cole, a former staff sergeant, lost most of her pension credit when she was awarded a war disablement pension because the Department for Work and Pensions treats military compensation as income for pension credit calculations.

> “Before receiving that compensation, Pauline had been in receipt of £76.96 a week in pension credit. After receiving that compensation, her pension credit collapsed to just £9.23 a week.”

Babarinde said military compensation is disregarded for universal credit but counted against pension credit, a quirk the Royal British Legion calls a breach of the armed forces covenant. He told the House the Department has said it has 'no plans to change this policy' and has not assessed the cost of fixing it, and asked the Minister to undertake a financial assessment and meet Pauline's sons before Report stage. The Minister for the Armed Forces, Al Carns, said in his closing reply that the Government would take the argument forward.
