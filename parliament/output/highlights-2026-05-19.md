# Hansard Highlights — 2026-05-19

*Commons · 15 highlights · compiled 2026-05-22T20:14:16Z*

> The Transport Secretary set out a reset of HS2, putting the cost of completion at £87.7 billion to £102.7 billion and the first London to Birmingham services between 2036 and 2039. An urgent question saw the Intelligence and Security Committee accuse the Government of redacting and withholding documents beyond the terms of the Mandelson Humble Address, and the fourth day of the King's Speech debate, on energy security, divided the House over the Bill to end new North Sea oil and gas licences.

### 1. New evidence — Heidi Alexander (Lab)

**Debate:** High Speed 2 Reset
**Time:** 2026-05-19T13:19:59

**Why it matters.** The Transport Secretary, Heidi Alexander, put the cost of completing HS2 at £87.7 billion to £102.7 billion and said the first trains between London and Birmingham would not run until between 2036 and 2039.

> “I can today confirm that the previous Government spent most of HS2's budget without laying a single metre of its track.”

Alexander published the Lovegrove report alongside HS2's revised cost and schedule. She attributed two thirds of the cost increase to underestimation and inefficiency and one third to inflation, and said the line's design speed would be cut to 320 kph to save up to £2.5 billion. She told the House that cancelling HS2 could cost almost as much as completing it.

### 2. Effective scrutiny — Sir Jeremy Wright (Con, Kenilworth and Southam)

**Debate:** Mandelson documents (Urgent Question)
**Time:** 2026-05-19T12:45:21

**Why it matters.** Sir Jeremy Wright (CON), chair of the Intelligence and Security Committee, said the Government intended to redact and withhold documents beyond the terms of the Mandelson Humble Address, which he called a question of parliamentary sovereignty.

> “It has become apparent to us that the Government also intend to redact documents for other reasons not specifically permitted in the Humble Address or, in some cases, to withhold documents altogether.”

The Humble Address agreed in February required documents on Peter Mandelson's appointment to be referred to the Intelligence and Security Committee, with redactions limited to national security or international relations grounds. Wright, the Committee's chair, said the Government was also redacting on other grounds and withholding some documents entirely, and asked it to seek the House's consent for any change. Darren Jones said the Government had complied with the Address and would publish a second tranche of material after the Whitsun recess.

**The debate around this — 3 voices:**

- *Minister's reply* — Darren Jones: “In response to the questions from the right hon. and learned Gentleman about compliance with the Humble Address, I refer him to the statement that I have made previously in relation to the principles set out in legislation and the motions of the House.”
- *Rebuttal* — Neil O’Brien: “if the Humble Address motion does not allow for redaction on those grounds, the Government cannot just unilaterally decide to ignore the will of the House”

### 3. Sharp exchange — Claire Coutinho (Con, East Surrey)

**Debate:** Debate on the Address: Energy Security
**Time:** 2026-05-19T00:00:00

**Why it matters.** The shadow Energy Secretary, Claire Coutinho, moved the Opposition's amendment against the planned ban on new North Sea oil and gas licences, arguing it would raise Britain's dependence on imported energy.

> “It is an energy dependence Bill that would leave us weaker, poorer and more reliant on foreign regimes.”

Coutinho moved the Opposition's amendment to the Humble Address, opposing the planned end to new North Sea oil and gas licences, the cancellation of a third large nuclear plant at Wylfa, and the carbon tax regime. She argued that cutting domestic production would not reduce consumption but increase imports. The Government's position is that the price of oil and gas is set on international markets, so new licences would not lower bills.

### 6. Effective scrutiny — Gregory Stafford (Con, Farnham and Bordon)

**Debate:** Jury Trial Proposals
**Time:** 2026-05-19T11:30:00

**Why it matters.** Gregory Stafford (CON) challenged the Justice Secretary over plans to restrict jury trials, citing Bar Council and Institute for Government estimates that the change would save little court time.

> “The backlog was not caused by juries and it will not be solved by scrapping juries.”

The Government's Courts and Tribunals Bill would restrict the right to a jury trial for some offences. Stafford cited the Bar Council and the Institute for Government, which estimated the change would save at most about 2% of court time, and argued the Crown court backlog was not caused by juries. Lammy said the backlog stood at more than 80,000 cases and that reform, alongside investment, was needed to reduce it.

### 8. Original argument — Katie Lam (Con, Weald of Kent)

**Debate:** Debate on the Address: Energy Security
**Time:** 2026-05-19T17:22:03

**Why it matters.** Katie Lam (CON) set out the case that more domestic gas production would lower household heating bills, on the basis that gas is a partly localised market.

> “If we produced more gas domestically, it would be cheaper to buy gas, meaning that heating bills would, in fact, come down.”

Lam argued that gas, particularly liquefied natural gas shipped from abroad, is priced partly by the distance it travels, so that more domestic production would lower the cost of supply to Britain. The Government and several Members cited the National Energy System Operator and the International Energy Agency in arguing that new North Sea production would not materially affect prices or security of supply. The exchange turned on whether domestic gas output affects the price British households pay.
