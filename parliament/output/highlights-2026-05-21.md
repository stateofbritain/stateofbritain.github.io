# Hansard Highlights — 2026-05-21

*Commons · 14 highlights · compiled 2026-05-21T20:48:46Z*

> Industrial and economic policy ran through the day: the Chancellor set out a package of measures in response to rising costs, with funds for the chemicals and ceramics industries, and the Commons gave a Second Reading to the Bill that would bring British Steel into public ownership. The Foreign Office also made statements on Iran, the Strait of Hormuz and the wider region.

### 1. Policy signal — Rachel Reeves (Lab, Leeds West and Pudsey)

**Debate:** Economic Statement

**Why it matters.** The Chancellor, Rachel Reeves, froze fuel duty, suspended tariffs on more than 100 foods and cut VAT on summer attractions, in a package funded by a change to the taxation of foreign branch profits.

> “Today I am taking action by suspending tariffs on over 100 different foods sold in supermarkets. I am clear that I expect supermarkets to pass those savings on in full to their customers.”

The Chancellor announced a freeze in fuel duty, a cut in red diesel duty, a higher tax-free mileage rate, the suspension of tariffs on more than 100 foods, a temporary VAT cut on summer attractions and children's meals, and funds of £350 million for chemicals and £120 million for ceramics. She said the package would be paid for by a change to the taxation of foreign branch profits, which the Government expects to raise hundreds of millions of pounds a year.

[Hansard](https://hansard.parliament.uk/Commons/2026-05-21/debates/674A538A-039E-488B-AEA3-24408F3E08BE/middle-east-economic-response#contribution-690C120F-375D-4C5E-86DB-1D3F34A51A6B)

**The debate around this — 3 voices:**

- *Opposition attack* — Sir Mel Stride: “the inevitable U-turn has finally come”
- *Liberal Democrat response* — Daisy Cooper: “For six months, we Liberal Democrats have been calling for an emergency temporary VAT cut for hospitality, leisure and attractions, and our pledge was fully funded.”
- *Question* — Kirsty Blackman: “have the Government modelled the job losses that may occur as a result of this change in the oil and gas taxation regime?”

### 2. Policy signal — Peter Kyle (Lab)

**Debate:** Steel Industry (Nationalisation) Bill

**Why it matters.** The Business Secretary, Peter Kyle, opened the Second Reading of the Bill that would bring British Steel into public ownership.

> “Our determination now is that the future may best be served by full public ownership in the national interest, not because of ideology, but because of practical pragmatism.”

The Bill gives Ministers power to bring steel companies, including British Steel at Scunthorpe, into public ownership where a public interest test is met. The Conservatives opposed it with a reasoned amendment. The Bill passed its Second Reading.

[Hansard](https://hansard.parliament.uk/Commons/2026-05-21/debates/D7727851-30C5-485A-B107-7E792BA527B9/steel-industry-nationalisation-bill#contribution-ED0EE196-82FE-4EBA-9973-16E411C00526)

**The debate around this — 3 voices:**

- *Scrutiny of the powers* — Sir Jeremy Wright: “Would the interests of one town where a steel facility is located be sufficient to justify the nationalisation of an entire company?”
- *Opposition amendment* — Andrew Griffith: “we do not believe that politicians or Whitehall bureaucrats should run businesses”
- *Minister winding up* — Chris McDonald: “More than 30 years ago—the House will not believe it—I left school and got a job in the steel industry.”

### 3. Effective scrutiny — Liam Byrne (Lab, Birmingham Hodge Hill and Solihull North)

**Debate:** Steel Industry (Nationalisation) Bill

**Why it matters.** Liam Byrne (LAB), who chairs the Business and Trade Committee, backed the Bill but said nationalisation would not succeed without lower energy costs, an EU tariff deal and firmer procurement.

> “although the Secretary of State is proposing some perhaps welcome statism, he must not forget the statecraft that is needed to make a success of this Bill”

Liam Byrne supported the Bill and set out six points: where investment for electric arc furnaces would come from, energy costs before the competitiveness scheme starts in 2027, a tariff deal with the EU, procurement, scrap supply, and consolidation of the industry.

### 4. Effective scrutiny — Stuart Anderson (Con, South Shropshire)

**Debate:** Steel Industry (Nationalisation) Bill

**Why it matters.** Stuart Anderson (CON) said the 50% steel tariff starting on 1 July would hit a major stainless steel firm in his constituency for grades Britain cannot make; the Business Secretary agreed to look into it.

> “The UK cannot meet domestic market demand, and a huge gap will be created. Businesses such as Amodil will be forced to import, and tariffs will drive up costs by 50%.”

Stuart Anderson said Amodil, a stainless steel stockholder in his constituency and one of the largest in the UK, holds 2,000 tonnes of steel that cannot be made in Britain, and that the new 50% tariff would price such firms out. Several Conservative MPs raised similar cases through the day. The Business Secretary said the tariffs were intended to protect domestic production, not goods the UK does not make, and undertook to look into the company personally.

### 5. Effective scrutiny — Mr Alistair Carmichael (LD, Orkney and Shetland)

**Debate:** Industrial Strategy

**Why it matters.** Alistair Carmichael (LD) asked why food manufacturing, the largest part of the manufacturing base, was excluded from the industrial strategy.

> “The largest sector of the manufacturing base is food manufacturing, but it was deliberately left out of the industrial strategy.”

Alistair Carmichael said food manufacturing faces rising input costs, especially energy, and cannot access the support the strategy offers other sectors, with food price inflation forecast to reach 9% or 10% this year. The Minister, Chris McDonald, said the strategy had selected the sectors with the greatest growth potential but that food manufacturing remained important. The point was raised again with the Chancellor later in the day.

[Hansard](https://hansard.parliament.uk/Commons/2026-05-21/debates/DAE668D4-F2E5-4183-AA38-020165545BCB/industrial-strategy#contribution-26797D20-F018-4333-8806-81B688A1F9D9)

**The debate around this — 2 voices:**

- *Minister's reply* — Chris McDonald: “At the time that the industrial strategy was established, the Government carefully selected those sectors that had the greatest growth potential for the economy”
- *Cross-party echo* — Sarah Bool: “Energy costs are driving up food inflation, as are a lot of the other anti-business policies put forward by this Government, including not incorporating food manufacturing in the industrial strategy.”

**Why it matters.** Sarah Olney (LD) said Government procurement, meant under the industrial strategy to back innovation, instead favours large and US suppliers over British start-ups.

### 9. Effective scrutiny — Laurence Turner (Lab, Birmingham Northfield)

**Debate:** Employment Rights Act 2025

**Why it matters.** Laurence Turner (LAB) told ministers a £500,000 cap on fines for breaching union access rules is 0.02% of the operating profit of Amazon's warehousing arm but a fifth of a mid-sized union's surplus.

> “Capping fines at £500,000 equates to a potential liability of 0.02% of operating profits for Amazon's warehousing operations, but up to 20% of the surplus of a medium-sized trade union.”

Laurence Turner said a £500,000 cap on penalties would be a trivial sum for a large employer like Amazon but a heavy one for a medium-sized union, and asked how that achieves a workable right of access. The Minister, Kate Dearden, said the enforcement mechanism must be fair and proportionate and would weigh the gravity of a breach and the size of the party.

[Hansard](https://hansard.parliament.uk/Commons/2026-05-21/debates/06324B25-3536-4B5F-A45F-3E00D51432B0/employment-rights-act-2025#contribution-733C3E46-D944-4F9A-A194-5C9E49809C97)

**The debate around this — 1 voice:**

### 11. Policy signal — Mr Hamish Falconer (Lab)

**Debate:** Middle East

**Why it matters.** The Foreign Office Minister, Hamish Falconer, updated the House on efforts to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, closed by Iran, and on Gaza and the wider region.

> “We cannot risk tens of millions of people going hungry because Iran has hijacked a vital international shipping lane.”

The Foreign Office Minister said Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz was a breach of international law driving a global food crisis, and set out a multinational naval mission to support shipping. ### 12. Crisis — Tony Vaughan (Lab, Folkestone and Hythe)

**Debate:** Imprisonment of Craig and Lindsay Foreman in Iran

**Why it matters.** Tony Vaughan (LAB) led an adjournment debate on Craig and Lindsay Foreman, two British tourists jailed in Iran for 10 years for espionage and now on hunger strike.

> “simply by speaking to people about what makes a good life, the Foremans were conducting espionage and attempting to overthrow the Iranian regime”

Tony Vaughan said Craig and Lindsay Foreman, the mother and stepfather of one of his constituents, were convicted by a revolutionary court after forced confessions and solitary confinement, and are now on hunger strike in Evin prison. Members from both sides asked the Government to declare the detention arbitrary. The Minister, Hamish Falconer, said the couple were innocent tourists but that public pressure was not always the most effective route to a release.
